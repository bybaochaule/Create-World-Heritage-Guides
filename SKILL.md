# Create World Heritage Guides

## Overview

Create accurate, educator-ready resources about UNESCO World Heritage Sites. Prioritize verified heritage facts, clear learning design, respectful interpretation, and audience-appropriate depth.

## Workflow

1. Identify the deliverable:
   - Full educational guide
   - Site profile or fact sheet
   - Lesson plan, classroom activity, quiz, or answer key
   - Museum label, exhibit panel, or public-history text
   - Documentary outline, narration beats, interview questions, or episode treatment
   - Travel education briefing or field-study handout

2. Clarify only missing essentials:
   - Site name, country, and whether the user means a specific component of a serial or transboundary property
   - Audience level, region/curriculum constraints, output length, and desired tone
   - Whether citations, handouts, tables, maps, slides, or quiz formats are required

3. Verify current site facts before presenting them as settled. Use official UNESCO World Heritage Centre pages first when browsing is available or when the user requests current data. If official verification is not possible, state the limitation and avoid exact claims that could be wrong.

4. Build the resource from the user's requested components. Use concise headings, classroom-ready wording, and concrete examples. Avoid generic travel-copy phrasing.

5. End with source notes or a bibliography when factual claims, dates, criteria, conservation status, or statistics appear in the output.

## Source Standards

Read `references/source-standards.md` when producing factual site data, current preservation status, citations, or anything that may have changed recently.

Required facts to verify when included:
- Official property name
- Country or countries
- Region
- Inscription year and extension years
- UNESCO criteria
- Property type: cultural, natural, or mixed
- UNESCO property ID when useful
- Component parts for serial or transboundary sites
- Coordinates, area, and buffer zone only if the output needs map or technical context
- Conservation state, risk factors, or Danger List status

Do not invent UNESCO criteria, inscription dates, boundaries, coordinates, threat status, management plans, or endangered status. Separate verified historical evidence, scholarly interpretation, local tradition, myth, and contested claims.

## Guide Components

Read `references/guide-templates.md` when the user asks for a full guide, multiple resource types, or a structured package.

Use these components as needed:
- Site profile: official name, location, type, criteria, inscription date, and concise overview
- Historical or geological context: major periods, cultures, events, ecosystems, or processes
- Significance: explain why the property has Outstanding Universal Value in plain language
- Map context: regional setting, nearby landmarks, route or landscape relationships, and spatial scale
- Timeline: 6 to 12 high-value entries, not a cluttered chronology
- Key facts: short, checkable facts suitable for educators or labels
- Preservation notes: threats, conservation responses, tourism pressure, climate risk, governance, and community roles
- Glossary: terms students will need, defined in accessible language
- Assessment: quiz questions, answer key, and optional short-answer rubric
- Discussion prompts: open-ended, evidence-based, culturally respectful
- Activities: grade-appropriate, feasible, and tied to learning objectives
- Museum labels: concise interpretive text with object/site relevance, date/context, and visitor takeaway
- Documentary outline: hook, sequence, scenes, narration beats, visuals, expert voices, and ethical considerations

## Audience Calibration

For elementary students, use concrete language, sensory observation, maps, simple timelines, and short activities.

For secondary students, include cause-and-effect, primary/secondary source thinking, preservation debates, and discussion prompts.

For university or adult learners, include historiography, heritage policy, conservation tradeoffs, contested interpretation, and deeper source notes.

For museum visitors, write labels that are brief, vivid, accurate, and centered on one interpretive idea.

For documentary creators, structure materials around story arcs, visuals, expert voices, permissions, cultural sensitivity, and fact-check checkpoints.

For travel educators, balance logistical map context with heritage interpretation; do not frame the guide as a tourism promotion unless the user asks.

## Quality Bar

Make every output:
- Accurate enough for an educator to reuse with confidence
- Clear about what is known, interpreted, debated, or changing
- Specific to the site, not a reusable generic heritage essay
- Respectful toward descendant, Indigenous, local, faith, and steward communities
- Useful as a teaching or interpretation artifact without extra rewriting

When the request is broad, deliver a complete first version with sensible assumptions and list those assumptions briefly.
